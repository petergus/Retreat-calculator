1. Lending concepts

TermPlain meaningAlso calledWhy it matters
LTV (Loan‑to‑Value) How much of the cost base the lender covers as a percent. Advance rate, leverage Higher LTV means less cash needed now, but more debt service later.
LTC (Loan‑to‑Cost) Percent of total project cost the lender will fund. Construction advance %, rehab leverage Some lenders cap loan by cost instead of value. Impacts how much you can borrow.
DSCR EBITDA divided by annual debt service. Debt coverage, coverage ratio Lenders want DSCR ≥ 1.20 to 1.35. Lower DSCR can mean no loan or a smaller loan.
Debt Yield EBITDA divided by total loan amount. DY Quick risk check for lenders. Many want ≥ 10 percent.
Amortization Schedule that pays interest and principal every year until the loan is zero. Amort, P&I Longer amort lowers payments but you pay more total interest.
Interest‑only You pay only interest for a period. Principal stays the same. I/O period Lowers early payments. But a big step-up when amort starts.
Fixed vs Variable Rate Fixed does not change. Variable moves with an index plus a margin. ARM, floating rate Variable can be cheaper at first but risky if rates rise.
Example: Loan A at 70 percent LTV and 6.8 percent for 20 years. Loan B at 65 percent LTV and 7.5 percent for 15 years.

2. Debt math and payments

Annual payment (amortized) = P × i ÷ (1 − (1+i)^−N), where P is principal, i is yearly rate, N is years.

Monthly version: use monthly rate and months as i = r/12, N = years × 12.

Blended payments: If you have two loans, total debt service is the sum of each loan's payment.

The calculator uses annual payments for clarity. You can convert to monthly if needed.

Common pitfalls

  • Comparing a 15‑year loan to a 25‑year loan without adjusting the payment basis.
  • Ignoring adjustable rate resets.
  • Forgetting balloon payments on short terms.

5. Escrows, reserves, and holdbacks

  • Escrow/Impound: Lender collects monthly for taxes and insurance.
  • Replacement reserve: You set money aside for big future items. In the calculator this is a percent of structures each year.
  • Operating reserve: Cash buffer at close, e.g., 3 to 6 months of expenses.
  • Construction holdback: Lender funds construction in draws as you build.
Tip: The calculator models replacement reserve in opex. If your lender requires an operating reserve at close, add it to CapEx as a custom line (not financed) so equity reflects it.

6. Income, NOI, EBITDA, cap rate, and value

Gross revenue = Lodging + Meals + Extras.

Net revenue ignores pass‑through taxes (you collect and remit).

Variable costs scale with guest‑nights (food, amenities).

Fixed opex are mostly the same each month (staff, utilities, insurance, property tax, maintenance, reserve).

EBITDA = Net revenue − all operating costs. No interest and no depreciation.

NOI (Net Operating Income) is similar to EBITDA here since we do not include non‑cash items.

Cap rate converts income into value. Value = EBITDA ÷ Cap rate.

Example: EBITDA 300,000. Exit cap 10 percent. Value ≈ 3,000,000.

LTV on value = Total loans ÷ Value. Shows leverage at exit.

7. US taxes you may face

  • Income tax: Federal and state on profits. Not modeled in the calculator.
  • Self‑employment/payroll taxes: On wages. We model the employer side via payroll burden percent.
  • Property tax: On assessed value of land and buildings. Modeled.
  • Sales tax: On meals or retail. Modeled.
  • Lodging/Occupancy tax: On room revenue. Modeled.
Names vary by state and county. “Lodging tax” may be called room tax, transient occupancy tax, hotel tax, accommodations tax, or bed tax.

8. Property tax and assessed value

Assessed value is the value the county uses for tax. It is not the market price. Some places assess a fraction of market value, then apply a mill rate.

In the calculator: Assessed = Purchase + Structures + Infrastructure + any custom CapEx you mark as “counts toward assessed”.

Property tax = Assessed × Property tax percent.

Example: Assessed 2,000,000. Rate 1.25 percent. Tax 25,000 per year.

Growth: We apply your yearly growth rate to build a 5‑year view.

9. Lodging, sales, and occupancy taxes

  • Split the nightly price into lodging and meals parts.
  • Lodging tax applies to the lodging part.
  • Sales tax applies to the meals part.
  • Price includes taxes? If yes, we back taxes out of the price. If no, taxes are added on top for the guest and we show collected amounts.
Example: PPPN 350. Meals 25 percent. Lodging tax 10 percent. Sales on meals 8.5 percent. If price includes taxes, the calculator solves the before‑tax price so your sticker stays 350.

11. Typical ranges and why they vary

  • LTV: 60 to 75 percent. Tighter for new operators or unusual properties.
  • DSCR: Lenders often want 1.20 to 1.35.
  • Debt yield: 8 to 12 percent common. Higher is safer for the bank.
  • Amort years: 15 to 25 typical for commercial hospitality.
  • Property tax: 0.5 to 2 percent of assessed, local rules vary.
  • EBITDA margin: 20 to 40 percent is a healthy target for small lodges.

Numbers shift with location, operator skill, marketing, and build quality.

12. What matters most for cash after debt

  • Price per person and occupancy move revenue the most.
  • Guests per night (design and layout) drives top line without big fixed costs.
  • OTA share and fees can eat margin fast.
  • Staff total and food costs are big levers.
  • Debt terms (rate, years) change payments a lot.
  • CapEx scope changes equity needs and future maintenance.
Rule of thumb: A 10 dollar price change at 10 guests and 60 percent occupancy is ≈ 21,900 more or less revenue per year (10 × 10 × 0.6 × 365).
